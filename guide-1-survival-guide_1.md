## Introduction: Why Lacrosse Is One of the Best Sports for Your Kid

Lacrosse is the fastest-growing team sport in America — and for good reason. It combines the athleticism of soccer, the strategy of basketball, and the teamwork of any great sport. Here's what parents of new players consistently say after their first season:

- **Kids love it.** There's almost no "bench-warming" in youth lacrosse. Everyone touches the ball.
- **It builds full-body athleticism.** Speed, agility, hand-eye coordination, spatial awareness — lacrosse develops all of it.
- **It's a real college pathway.** Lacrosse programs are actively recruiting at hundreds of colleges, including Ivy League schools.
- **The culture is fantastic.** Lacrosse communities tend to be tight-knit, positive, and welcoming to new families.

You don't need to know a single thing about lacrosse to start this journey. That's what this guide is for.

## Chapter 1: Should My Kid Play Lacrosse?

### The Honest Pros & Cons

**Pros:**
- Great cardiovascular and full-body workout
- Lower contact than football, still physically engaged
- Smaller team sizes = more playing time and attention from coaches
- Rapidly growing sport = more scholarship opportunities ahead
- Strong community and sportsmanship culture
- Year-round opportunity (indoor and outdoor seasons)

**Cons:**
- Equipment costs upfront (we'll tackle this in Chapter 2)
- Learning curve is real — new vocabulary, new rules
- Some programs require significant time commitment (travel teams especially)
- Field space can be limited in some areas

**The bottom line:** If your child is curious, athletic, and looking for something exciting — lacrosse is worth the leap. Most kids who try it don't go back.

---

### What Age to Start?

| Age | What to Expect |
|-----|----------------|
| 4–6 | "Lacrosse for Tots" — fun, no pressure, basic stick skills |
| 7–9 | Mini-lacrosse, modified rules, learning to catch and throw |
| 10–12 | Real team play, positions introduced, game rules apply |
| 13+ | Competitive leagues, travel teams, high school prep |

**Coach Zach's Note:** "Starting late is totally fine. I've seen kids pick up a stick at 14 and earn college scholarships. Don't let late entry discourage you."

## Chapter 2: Equipment on a Budget

### What's Actually Required vs. Nice-to-Have

Here's the honest breakdown. Many families spend way too much in year one on gear they don't need.

#### ✅ REQUIRED (You Cannot Play Without These)

| Item | Estimated Cost | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Helmet | $40–$120 | Must be NOCSAE-certified. Check used sports equipment stores. |
| Lacrosse stick (complete) | $30–$90 | Starter sticks are totally fine for year 1. |
| Gloves | $20–$50 | Don't skip these — they protect hands. |
| Arm pads | $15–$35 | Required for boys. Girls rules differ. |
| Shoulder pads (boys) | $20–$50 | Boys only. Girls lacrosse has different requirements. |
| Mouthguard | $5–$15 | Mandatory. Stock up on a few. |
| Cleats | $20–$60 | Soccer or football cleats work fine. |
| Athletic cup (boys) | $10–$20 | Don't skip this. |

**Realistic Year 1 Budget (Boys):** $160–$440
**Realistic Year 1 Budget (Girls):** $80–$200 (less protective gear required)

---

#### ⭐ NICE-TO-HAVE (But Not Required in Year 1)

- Extra sticks (nice if they break, but not urgent)
- Stick bag or gear bag ($30–$80)
- Rebounder net for home practice ($60–$150)
- Team-branded gear (wait until you know they're committed)

**Coach Zach's Money-Saving Tip:** "Hit Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp, and your local Play It Again Sports before buying new. Kids grow fast and gear gets barely used. You can outfit a beginner for under $100 if you're patient."

---

### Buying the Stick: What You Need to Know

For beginners, **do not overthink the stick**. Here's what matters:

- **Length:** Check your league's age guidelines (usually on their website)
- **Complete stick:** Buy a complete stick (head + shaft together) — not separate
- **Pocket:** Factory-strung pockets are fine for year one
- **Avoid:** High-end titanium shafts and custom stringing for first-year players

## Chapter 3: Tryouts, First Practice & First Season Survival

### Before Tryouts

- **Call the coach or club contact** and ask: "What should my child work on before tryouts?" They will tell you. They want to help.
- Let your child know tryouts are about learning, not performing perfectly
- Arrive 10–15 minutes early with all required gear
- Bring water and snacks

### What Coaches Are Actually Watching at Youth Tryouts

*From Coach Zach's direct experience running tryout evaluations:*

1. **Coachability** — Does the child listen and try what's being taught?
2. **Effort** — Are they giving full energy, even when tired?
3. **Attitude** — Are they being a good teammate?
4. **Natural athleticism** — Can they run, change direction, track a ball?
5. **Skill** — This matters less than the above, especially for new players

> "I would take a coachable, hard-working kid with no lacrosse experience over a skilled kid with a bad attitude every single time." — Coach Zach

---

### First Practice Survival Tips

- **Arrive early** the first few times so your child can get comfortable with the space
- **Pack a full water bottle** — lacrosse practices are intense
- **Let the coach coach** — resist the urge to call out instructions from the sideline
- **Ask your child after practice:** "What was the hardest part? What was fun?" Not: "How did you do?"
- **Expect frustration** — lacrosse skills take time. Two to three weeks in, most kids turn a corner.

### First Season Reality Check

| Week | What to Expect |
|------|----------------|
| 1–2 | Overwhelmed, unsure. Some kids want to quit. Stay calm. |
| 3–4 | Skills starting to click. Excitement building. |
| Mid-season | Getting into a rhythm. Starting to understand the game. |
| Late season | Visible growth. Confidence up. Already asking about next season. |

**For parents:** The mid-season slump is real and normal. Don't pull your child when things get hard. The breakthrough almost always comes shortly after.

## Chapter 4: Common Parent Mistakes (From Real Club Experience)

*These are the patterns Coach Zach has observed in thousands of families. They come from love — but they work against your child.*

### Mistake #1: Over-Coaching From the Sideline
Your voice competes with the coach's voice and confuses your child. Cheer loudly, but save feedback for the car ride home — and even then, lead with what they did well.

### Mistake #2: Comparing to Other Kids
Every player develops on a different timeline. The kid who dominates at age 10 isn't always the star at 16. Focus on your child's individual progress.

### Mistake #3: Making Their Bad Games About You
If they had a rough game, they already know it. They don't need a post-game debrief. They need a snack and "I love watching you play."

### Mistake #4: Overspending on Gear Too Early
New families buy top-of-the-line everything in month one. Then the kid decides lacrosse isn't for them. Start modest. Upgrade when they're truly committed.

### Mistake #5: Skipping Practice "Just This Once"
Consistency builds skills. One missed practice puts your child behind in drills and team concepts. Keep the commitment as much as possible — your child is watching how seriously you take it.

### Mistake #6: Talking Negatively About Coaches Around Your Child
Even quiet complaints at home trickle down. Kids who hear parents criticize coaches tend to stop listening to those coaches. Raise concerns privately and directly.

## Chapter 5: Supporting Your Player Without the Stress

### The Magic Phrase
Studies on youth sports psychology consistently point to one thing kids most want to hear from their parents:

> **"I love watching you play."**

That's it. No critique. No analysis. Just that.

---

### Building a Healthy Lacrosse Culture at Home

- **Let them lead the pace.** Don't pressure extra practice sessions unless they ask.
- **Celebrate effort, not outcome.** "You worked so hard on that drill" beats "you scored!"
- **Stay curious together.** Watch a college lacrosse game together on YouTube. Learn the rules alongside them.
- **Protect their love of the sport.** Burnout happens when sport feels like obligation. Keep it fun.

### When They Want to Quit

Almost every lacrosse player has a "I want to quit" moment. Before reacting:

1. Ask them to describe what specifically isn't fun
2. Listen without fixing
3. Give it 2–3 weeks before making any decisions
4. Talk to the coach privately if you're concerned

If quitting is still the answer after honest reflection — that's okay too. The goal is a child who grows, not a child who plays lacrosse.

### 🚨 Injury Red Flags — When to Pull Your Child
Get these checked by a medical professional:
- Head impact with any confusion, headache, dizziness, or vision changes (concussion protocol)
- Pain in joints that worsens with rest or overnight
- Any "pop" sound followed by swelling
- Limping that doesn't improve within 10–15 minutes
- Numbness or tingling in any limb
- Eye injury from a stick or ball

**Coach Zach's Rule:** "When in doubt, sit them out. No game is worth a serious injury."
